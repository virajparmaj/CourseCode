There are 3 bmi larger than 52 and the fourth largest is 48, so these may be outliers. We can remove these outliers to improve the performance.

The term outlier is usually subjective. The common practice is to ignore the outliers first, train a model, evaluate it, and if the model performance doesn't reach expectation, we will then try to apply some outlier detections. This is sometimes called winsorizing, and should be carefully documented in the analysis if it is used.

In field of insurance, there is an additional reason to not drop outliers, as we are interested in modeling the extreme cases, and those outliers may be the exact indicator of the accidents.

4. Risk Factor Analysis¶

As stated above, by the linear model, the most important feature is smoker or not, while the following features are regions (within categorical variables). By the Random Forst model, the feature importance states that smoker or not is still the most important feature, followed by bmi and age, which coincide with our descriptive analysis. To that sense, whether the policyholder smoking or not is critical to their claim experience, and it can be captured by both linear regression and tree-based models.

General interpretation: If the policyholders smoke, the predicted claims are higher; if the policyholder are older, the predicted claims are higher; if the policyholder have higher bmi, the predicted claims are higher.
